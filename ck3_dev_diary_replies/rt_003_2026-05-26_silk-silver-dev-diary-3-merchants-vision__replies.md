## Reply 93 — participant_057 · 2026-05-26 · post-31315085

Im so stoked for the merchant and trade! Just a comment on some questions regarding Christian and Islamic prohibition of usury. One way of circumventing this, which gained increasing use at the beginning of the 13th century in Europe (but there are documented foreign exchange from mid 12th century), was the cambium contract. The merchant deposited money with his bank, the bank issued a credit note/bill which instructed a partner bank in foreign location and foreign currency to pay a specific sum to the merchant or his courier. Removing the need to carry vast sums of money or valuables with you. The bank profited from this by means of adjusting the exchange rate of the foreign currency (when the merchant cashed in the credit note) and so you have credit issued that is to be repaid at an expense of a small "exchange fee" at some later time - in effect an interest rate. In late 13th century Enrico da Susa (bishop and expert on canon law) argued that if you lend money to someone you have the right to receive compensation for the profits you could have made if you had invested the money yourself. By no means was this the end of the discussion about interest rates but it slowly shifted, started to gain acceptance. Suftaja and hawala, the Islamic equivalent, preceded the cambium contract by centuries and also had workarounds that were in effect interest rates. Exchange rate adjustment or simply fees. And just as in christian regions this wasn't without controversy.
