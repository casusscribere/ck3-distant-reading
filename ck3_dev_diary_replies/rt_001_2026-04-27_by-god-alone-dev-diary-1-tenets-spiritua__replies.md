> lachek said: Here I want to take a second to correct something I wrote last time: "Think of it as the spiritual counterpart to Stress, except instead of measuring how close you are to a mental breakdown, it measures how close you are to either sainthood or damnation." That is not entirely accurate, so to be extra precise: it measures how close you feel you are to being blessed in the Lord versus being slated for eternal damnation - or, in another belief system, your karmic balance, or how in tune you are to the Great Spirit. Naturally, this inner spiritual turmoil rubs off on how you behave towards people around you as well to some degree, but it is not something others judge you for - any penance necessary is all yours to administer upon yourself. This in turn has an effect on Stress, as well as other conditions, described below. This is in contrast to Piety, which measures how much the rest of your faith respects you as a figure of religious standing. You could very well choose an event option that yields Piety for your external display while dropping your Spiritual Fulfillment because it doesn't correspond with what your Faith (or, more precisely, your chosen Rite) believes to be right living. More commonly, an option that adds Stress because it conflicts with your Traits, but also adds Spiritual Fulfillment because it's the virtuous thing to do. For Christian characters, Fulfillment steps through seven named tiers, each with its own unlocks. At the very bottom is Doomed (-95 and below), where stress gain is cranked up substantially but the gloves are also off: murder schemes do not generate stress, hostile schemes against clergy are similarly worry-free, and you can fabricate hooks on your own Court Chaplain. The worst place to be spiritually, but in some ways the most liberating place politically. Above that sits Forsaken (-65), which still gives you free rein to plot against your clergy, but eases off the stress-gain multiplier. Troubled (-30) retains only the Chaplain-hook permission - you're still sinning, but now you're noticing. Penitent (0) is the neutral midpoint, with nothing unlocked and nothing penalized; you're at peace with yourself, if not with God. Absolved (+30) starts charging you extra Fulfillment for hostile schemes against clergy and lets you run Befriend on your Chaplain. Settled (+65) boosts your stress drain, extends Befriend to your Head of Rite, and opens up the new Act of Devotion decision. And finally, at In Grace (+95), you get the full kit: further improved stress reduction, and access to the Mentor in Spiritual Fulfillment decision which lets you guide other characters through their own spiritual journeys. What we're hoping this produces is exactly the kind of dilemma we've been talking about. A Generous, Pious ruler who climbs to In Grace feels great and has tools other characters don't, but has also made it costlier to scheme against the Church. A Doomed or Forsaken ruler has more levers to pull and stronger nerves for pulling them, but suffer more for going against their nature. Low Fulfillment isn't a failure state - it's just a different playstyle, and depending on your character's personality it might even be the more coherent one. Click to expand...

I am going to nitpick here: he doesn't "know". He is of a conviction, that might be true, but since there is no link between the two, he doesn't know. This might sound like semantics, but a character's spiritual intuition should be dynamic and not just "sins are going to make me be punished". If a character as a child sees other characters who sin rise to great heights or virtous ones fall, they might be more sceptical on theology. A character's personality should influence what faith they will follow, rather than just be a "good luck/ you are doomed" fixed stat. How will this interact with characters with the (pre-existing) "atheist" or "cynical" personality type/trait? Say a character, for whatever reason, becomes less convinced of god, will they ignore this system or will they just suffer stress from fear of a punishment that they don't believe in? While the vast majority of people in the middle ages was religious, in the higher echelons of secular society there were still people who were at the very least apathetic to the idea of god (similar to the stance of voltaire and epicurus) or were deist, and it already is a personality trait Also, the effects on behavior seem lacklustre. A ruler that is convinced he is likely to go to hell (but not guaranteed) should be doing his absolute best to get in god's good graces, particularly if he is old or sick (fullfillment should at least influence last rites), and act more cowardly, risk-averse and defensively in geopolitics, except holy wars of course, because he fears god supporting his enemy. I think the simplest and most elegant way to fix most of the issues around easily hitting caps like stress gain/loss or character opinions (hitting -100 or +100 with almost everyone, suddenly rendering all opinion mods beyond that cutoff irrelevant) would be to make them all multiplicative, rather than additive, when stacked. So, for instance, if you have +50 and -20 and -10 stress gain, they can cancel out and leave you with just the one net +20% gain. But if after cancelling you still end up with a -50 and a -20 and three -10 stress gain reductions, you shouldn't be literally immune to stress, as you are now; that's absurd, and causes all kinds of issues because the game is designed around the assumption that stress matters. And it's not hard to find such huge stress gain reductions of 50% or even more, hidden in certain decisions, as scaling bonuses based on piety levels, at least one legend event chain, etc. Whereas if they were multiplicative, your total stress gain rate would be .5 * .8 * .9 * .9 * .9 = 29.16%, rather than zero. Which is still a big reduction, just not to the point of invalidating a whole huge portion of the game vital to overall balance, one which is supposed to affect both the most powerful and the weakest characters alike. This would have a few benefits: no matter how low a net modifier drops, it's always worth seeking out more modifiers to reduce it even further (because -100 is now an asymptote rather than a simple cap). The biggest reductions are still worth the cost and risk; getting another 50% reduction at this point would drop you from 29.16% to 14.58% stress gain, a decent improvement and exactly the same in a relative sense, rather than being utterly worthless as it is in the current system. More importantly, there would be little need to rebalance existing reward values, and future added events or mods could be accommodated easily and continue being balanced more or less in a vacuum, because it is self-balancing to a degree as diminishing returns are automatically baked in to the calculations.

## Reply 281 — participant_123 · 2026-05-03 · post-31267663

For some reason I can't reply to you, @Kingsaver, but I'll attempt to explain the practical difference between Tenet, Rite and Doctrines in-game using examples A TENET is things like Armed Pilgrimages, Carnal Exaltation, Pursuit of Power, Legalism, Sanctity of Nature, that kind of thing. They basically are the belief system of your faith. A DOCTRINE is things like views on gender (Male Dominated, Equal or Female Dominated), Religious Attitude (Fundamentalist, Righteous or Pluralist), Clerical Tradition (Theocratic or Lay Clergy), Head of Faith (None, Temporal or Spiritual), Theism (Monotheist or Polytheist) among others. Its how your belief system works. A RITE is like a subdivision of the faith, still in communion with it, such as the Insular faith still recognizing the Catholic faith's (now renamed to Latin Rite) Pope in Rome as their Head of Faith. The faith may have some Tenet and Doctrinal differences, but as long as it isn't TOO divergent, then its allowed, doesn't go against or contradict core beliefs, its alright. If you want a real life example, the Eastern Catholic Churches, such as the Maronites, work a little different than the mainline Roman Catholic Church, but they all still recognize the Pope in Rome as the leader of the Church.
