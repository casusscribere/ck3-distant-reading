## Reply 28 — participant_022 · 2026-04-21 · post-31244215

Would we have multiple assemblies within a Republic? Like venice have multiple different assemblies existign alongside each other, with different assemblies being open to people of different status and class. What about the magistrates beneath the Signora or the Doge? Venice had sages which act as ministers, and eventually they became more powerful than the Doge which can be a figurehead at times. Regarding trade and goods. One thing I want to know is if settlement size is shaped by how much resources/food you can import to a city. A city cut off from trade will likely soon lose its allure and its settlement size as people can't find enough food available to sustain a city. Cutting off, or diverting trade routes away from a city could really weaken the city strategically even without warfare. Another thing is trade, especially luxury trade often piggyback off bulk-trade routes. A big ship shipping grain en bulk to Constantinople will be cheaper for a luxury trade merchant to hop onto the spare space on the ship and ride along for the journey, making the overall cost of transportation lower and easier. So it's not just a matter of competing trade routes, but trade routes can supplement each other because people can rely on the infrastructure ( road networks, regular shipping convoys) to make trade cheaper and easier for other kinds of produce.

Districts & Buildings Before anything else, we should discuss buildings and districts. I have already mentioned districts a couple of times throughout the dev diary, so it is time for a clarification. We often found it weird that, as a ruler, you would build a brewery or a windmill. It always felt a bit underwhelming, and with few building slots, it was hard to dig deeper into the system, as there wasn’t a lot of room to add anything. As part of our rework, we have removed every single regular building in the game (note, we have mostly kept all the special buildings that existed, but they will all see tweaks to their effects). Instead of having building slots, we will now have district slots instead, with districts in them. A district is effectively a thematic folder for buildings, where you will build a district (which will have some effects by itself), and then you will be able to build buildings within the district afterward. Instead of building an e.g., a smithy, you will be able to build a district for artisans that you can then fill with different buildings like a smithy. The goal is that a district is an investment, whereas the buildings are more easily switched and cheaper to replace if need be, and they will be flavorful and thematic. So you will see districts focused on the military, markets, urban life, or even on keeping the city small so it can remain an effective rural location. Speaking of size… View attachment 1488067​ Development Changes Development was always meant to symbolize both how wealthy an area is and how many people live there. It could lead to some weird results, where one would be conflated with the other, and vice versa. So, for Silk & Silver, we are splitting development into two: “Settlement Size” and “Prosperity”. Prosperity is a measure of how rich and prosperous a county is, and it will affect how likely traders are to drop by (as they will make more money there), as well as multiplying the amount of tax obtained from the county. Settlement Size is a measure of how many people live in the city in the barony (a county capital will differ from other holdings, for example). We will use a 1-10 scale to track size in a somewhat abstract way. Size will directly affect how many district slots are available in the province, so you will, for example, see a whole lot more districts in Constantinople than you would in, e.g., the capital of Sweden. But in turn, we have added new rural districts that will benefit from smaller sizes, while counties with larger settlements face challenges to both county control and popular opinion within the county. Control Changes County control will no longer always max out at 100, as it used to before. We will now have a new baseline system, where different things will add to the baseline control you can have, to showcase that different counties and districts work differently. Your capital will always have relatively high control, as this is where you are situated and live, but your other titles will need a bit of… encouragement to actually pay taxes and the like. You will be able to gain control from stationing Men-at-Arms, building castles and fortifications in the area, from different eras, and most likely, some more districts will give bonuses to control as well. Since this system works a bit like, e.g., Court Grandeur, you can gain effects that temporarily increase it beyond the baseline. For example, let us say your current baseline is 40; that is now the number control will always trickle towards. Then you can gain effects that could bump up your current control to 60 or 80, but it would then trickle down towards 40 again, as that is only a temporary boon to the area. The goal is that it will take a bit more investment to ensure that a county is worthwhile conquering, and that holding more land in itself is not useful if the people who live there aren’t incentivized to give you any taxes. Popular Opinion changes For Popular Opinion, we are using the same baseline changes we have for control, but instead, it is now a bar from -100 to 100. Similarly to control, it means you can now gain a temporary popular opinion that will bump the current opinion up or down, but it will trickle back towards what the popular opinion is at any given time. Unlike before, there will now also be positive effects from having a positive level of popular support. Higher levels of popular opinion will have a direct effect on control in an area, which means that you will, in turn, gain more from the county as a whole. It could lead to some weird results, where one would be conflated with the other, and vice versa. So, for Silk & Silver, we are splitting development into two: “Settlement Size” and “Prosperity”. Prosperity is a measure of how rich and prosperous a county is, and it will affect how likely traders are to drop by (as they will make more money there), as well as multiplying the amount of tax obtained from the county. Settlement Size is a measure of how many people live in the city in the barony (a county capital will differ from other holdings, for example). We will use a 1-10 scale to track size in a somewhat abstract way. Size will directly affect how many district slots are available in the province, so you will, for example, see a whole lot more districts in Constantinople than you would in, e.g., the capital of Sweden. But in turn, we have added new rural districts that will benefit from smaller sizes, while counties with larger settlements face challenges to both county control and popular opinion within the county. Control Changes County control will no longer always max out at 100, as it used to before. We will now have a new baseline system, where different things will add to the baseline control you can have, to showcase that different counties and districts work differently. Your capital will always have relatively high control, as this is where you are situated and live, but your other titles will need a bit of… encouragement to actually pay taxes and the like. You will be able to gain control from stationing Men-at-Arms, building castles and fortifications in the area, from different eras, and most likely, some more districts will give bonuses to control as well. Since this system works a bit like, e.g., Court Grandeur, you can gain effects that temporarily increase it beyond the baseline. For example, let us say your current baseline is 40; that is now the number control will always trickle towards. Then you can gain effects that could bump up your current control to 60 or 80, but it would then trickle down towards 40 again, as that is only a temporary boon to the area. The goal is that it will take a bit more investment to ensure that a county is worthwhile conquering, and that holding more land in itself is not useful if the people who live there aren’t incentivized to give you any taxes. Popular Opinion changes For Popular Opinion, we are using the same baseline changes we have for control, but instead, it is now a bar from -100 to 100. Similarly to control, it means you can now gain a temporary popular opinion that will bump the current opinion up or down, but it will trickle back towards what the popular opinion is at any given time. Unlike before, there will now also be positive effects from having a positive level of popular support. Higher levels of popular opinion will have a direct effect on control in an area, which means that you will, in turn, gain more from the county as a whole. Strengths of the Design​Splitting Development into Settlement Size and Prosperity — this is the correct solution to a long-standing structural problem. The current system conflates two fundamentally different concepts: wealth and population. Separating them allows for meaningful asymmetry — a large but poor city, or a small but highly prosperous торговый центр. Districts instead of buildings — conceptually solid. The current building system is just flat modifiers. Districts introduce functional specialization: military, trade, agrarian. That’s actual depth, not just more buttons. Baseline for County Control — removing automatic 100% control after conquest is the right move. Holding land now requires investment. This directly hits blobbing and makes expansion materially costly. Assembly in republics — law voting with a gradual narrowing of participants from a broad commune to a narrow signoria. This is historically grounded and mechanically elegant. It’s an organic transformation, not a binary switch. Trade leagues — confederations of republics with shared protection and economic alignment. A Hanseatic-style system as a mechanic is a strong addition. Risks and Potential Issues​~900 markets — this is a simulation load problem. Each duchy acting as its own market with goods, needs, and traders implies a massive number of calculations. Considering that the map has already expanded significantly, late-game performance (1200–1300+) is a serious concern. This needs stress testing. “Building needs” risk becoming puzzle optimization — the described mechanic suggests buildings perform better if supplied with specific goods. For landed rulers, this risks becoming: “find the correct goods → get the bonus.” If the optimal setup is static, this is not a dynamic economy, it’s a checklist. A real system requires instability — supply disruptions, competition, and wars must actively break supply chains. Landless merchants — a fragile gameplay loop Gold → Prestige → more Gold is structurally clean, but incomplete. Merchants lack: armies vassals coercive leverage If Prestige does not translate into real power — debt pressure, political influence, or control over rulers — merchants will feel like participants without agency. Historically, banking families controlled rulers through debt. That needs to exist as a mechanic, not flavor text. Full building wipe — a nuclear but understandable decision. Design-wise: correct. Practically: disruptive. All existing mods with custom buildings will break. All saves become incompatible. Acceptable for a major expansion, but only if modders get proper documentation in advance. Modifier fragmentation → tooltip bloat Moving from general modifiers to highly specific ones is the correct direction. But if players are exposed to dozens of modifier types, readability collapses. At that point, it’s not depth — it’s UI noise. Popular Opinion (-100 to 100) affecting control — mechanically interesting, but philosophically unstable. Feudal logic: Now coexists with: Key questions: What happens at -50 Popular Opinion? How are taxes affected? How does this interact with vassal obligations? If these layers aren’t clearly separated, the system becomes internally inconsistent. Guelphs vs Ghibellines via Situations — known limitation: Situations decay over time. Additionally: geographically narrow (Northern Italy) low systemic impact outside the region This is flavor content, not a core mechanic. Two parallel DLCs — risk of disconnection By God Alone and Silk & Silver are released in the same cycle. Critical issue: trade is historically tied to religion (diasporas, restrictions, privileges). If: economic systems ≠ religious systems then a major part of historical reality becomes mechanically invisible. If this continues, the result will be a collection of disconnected mechanics rather than a cohesive simulation. Conclusion​This is the most ambitious systemic change Paradox has attempted in CK3. It touches: economy buildings control opinion The scope is correct. The risks are real. Key questions: Does the system hold up performance-wise with ~900 markets in late game? Are supply chains actually dynamic and unstable? Do landless merchants have real political leverage? Can players understand the system without parsing excessive tooltip complexity? There is also a broader, recurring design issue: If trade ends up being just another income multiplier, this DLC will fail.

Actually this is also how many Hanze cities operated. In northern Europe shipping was timed carefully to match the domestic market. This is an area of expertise for me, as a (former) historian (and I transcribe historical sailing records as a hobby). Cities like Venice, Genoa, Brugge, Groningen, etc. stored goods and released to market at more convenient times. A portion would be sold directly but if ships arrived with cargo and dumped it all on the market, the price for whatever they carried would quickly become lower due to market saturation and spike due to shortages later on, making the market volatile. Imports were often seasonal (partially due to harvest timing, partially due to weather conditions on shipping lanes) so it would be beneficial to store goods to maintain a stable price point, which is good for business. Some local products had to be sold in the local city first (for example Groningen decreed all grain to be sold in the city, to benefit local brewers). The best time to arrive with an exotic (spice) cargo was at the end of the year, just before the winter weather made travel more difficult. This did not only apply to ships which might get caught in storms, but also to merchants who had to travel to markets to buy these goods - once the roads became wet the transport of cargo became difficult. Paired with this is the shortening of days; it was considered unsafe to travel at dark, especially with precious cargo, for reasons of crime and poor visibility for navigation so merchants had to get their trading over with prior to this period. The timed arrival of certain trade fleets led to some interesting things which we still see in modern times: we associate spices like nutmeg, cinnamon, pepper, ginger, etc. with the fall and holiday seasons, which is when an influx of these goods would appear on the market. Special celebrations were seen as occasions where you could show your wealth and generosity, as well as splurge a little on luxuries (and not, contrary to popular myth, to cover up the quality of bad meat - meat was cheaper than spices and the quality of food was VERY heavily regulated by the local guilds). People often think of the middle ages as a rather chaotic and disorganized time but merchants generally had a strong understanding of how markets worked and knew that you needed to control/regulate them to a certain degree. This is why they generally cooperated and I am happy to see this reflected in the upcoming changes. I really hope there will be options for storage, controlled sales and price management to benefit multiple merchants, as this was more commonly seen than the cutthroat business practices of some Italian families.

There are three big known types in history, from ancient to medieval to early modern - A - Provinces - All the larger republics had centrally-governed administrative provinces with governors elected by the senate. From Rome and Carthage of the ancient age (especially Rome), to medieval ones like Novgorod, as well as Pisan province of Corsica, Genoese province of Gazaria (Crimea), Venetian provinces of Candia (Crete) and Morea (Peloponnese) and so on. B - Vassal Republics - A common running theme across history where regardless of size, many republics would often have smaller city-states as their nominally independent/autonomous vassal republics in an area. Be it distant colonies tied to metropoles in ancient Greece, then the numerous vassal cities of Greece and Italy under the Roman Republic (giving rise to the original idea of 'free cities' within a larger empire), to medieval Venice turning and holding Ragusa, Dyrrachium, Athens and others as its vassal states. Republic of Florence is also a good example - it recreated the original duchy of Tuscany (which had been dissolved after death of Matilda of Tuscany) over centuries by fighting, vassalizing and then directly controlling Siena, Pisa, Arrezzo and other cities in the area. C - Family States - Much like standard feudal vassals. As in, one family could conquer and hold a title and land as its own hereditary property (and thus a part of the republic but not under government control), doubling as feudal lords in that area while remaining usual politicians of the republic back at home. This was how it often worked in CK2 republics.
