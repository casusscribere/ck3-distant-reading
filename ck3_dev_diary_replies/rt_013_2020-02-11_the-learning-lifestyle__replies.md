## Reply 33 — participant_005 · 2020-02-11 · post-26245613

<!-- artifact:quote:start -->
> Nyrael said: Hm, it would be strange if Immortality meant "You age until a certain point, after which you just stop". It makes far more sense to me that you either stop aging when you achieve immortality, or that you never stop aging and later look like a ghoul. Click to expand...
<!-- artifact:quote:end -->
We're talking about pure fiction of course, but "ageing" isn't a linear focus (at least for humans). To simplify, the first step is for a human to grow to its adult stage ; then there's a "plateau", and then the process of senescence come in. Initial growth is a completely different process from what happens later on. It would make sense if immortality just stopped when reaching the "plateay" of adult age, when the body is fully formed, and granted a regeneration power to avoid senescence. That's actually the form of immortality that is discusses by scientists and would be achievable if we manage to cancel cell ageing. But there's also "undead" immortality, that is your body basically stops living but you still inhabit it. That way it doesn't age at all and you remain a child if you started your immortality as a child. That's often how vampires work in fiction (when they aren't changelings). And finally there's the eternal ageing solution, imo the least convincing one because it basically means that a spirit is trapped in a body that never stops ageing - so even if you should be dead, you're not, for some reason. There are examples of all three in fiction and besides personal preferences I don't think there's a better solution, as long as it's consistent with the rest of the world of fiction. Of course there are also several kinds of reincarnations (some examples are found in CK2) but it's another kind of immortality.
